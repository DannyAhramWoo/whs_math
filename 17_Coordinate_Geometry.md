## 1. Distance and Midpoint Formulas

- **Distance formula** (from Pythagoras): distance between (x‚āĀ,y‚āĀ) and (x‚āā,y‚āā) = ‚ąö[(x‚āā‚ąíx‚āĀ)¬≤ + (y‚āā‚ąíy‚āĀ)¬≤].
  - Example: distance from (1,1) to (4,5) = ‚ąö[3¬≤ + 4¬≤] = ‚ąö25 = 5.
- **Midpoint formula:** midpoint of (x‚āĀ,y‚āĀ) and (x‚āā,y‚āā) = ((x‚āĀ+x‚āā)/2, (y‚āĀ+y‚āā)/2).
  - Example: midpoint of (2,4) and (8,10) = (5,7).

---

## 2. Slope

- **Slope** m = (y‚āā‚ąíy‚āĀ)/(x‚āā‚ąíx‚āĀ) ‚ÄĒ measures steepness. Positive slope rises left-to-right, negative falls, zero slope is horizontal (y=k), and a vertical line (x=h) has undefined slope.
  - Example: through (2,3) and (6,11): slope = (11‚ąí3)/(6‚ąí2) = 8/4 = 2.

> ūüí° **Competition point:** Never swap the order of subtraction between numerator and denominator ‚ÄĒ always use the SAME point order in both.

---

## 3. Equations of a Line

- **Slope-intercept form:** y = mx + b (m = slope, b = y-intercept).
- **Point-slope form:** y ‚ąí y‚āĀ = m(x ‚ąí x‚āĀ), built directly from the slope formula using one known point.
- **Standard form:** Ax + By = C.
- **x-intercept / y-intercept:** set y=0 to find the x-intercept, set x=0 to find the y-intercept. Quick way to graph a line ‚ÄĒ just plot both intercepts and connect them.
  - Example: for 2x + 3y = 12, x-intercept = (6,0), y-intercept = (0,4).

---

## 4. Parallel & Perpendicular Lines

- **Parallel lines** have EQUAL slopes.
- **Perpendicular lines** have slopes that are **negative reciprocals** of each other (their product = ‚ąí1).
  - Example: a line perpendicular to slope 2/3 has slope ‚ąí3/2.

---

## 5. Equation of a Circle

- A circle with center (h,k) and radius r has equation (x‚ąíh)¬≤ + (y‚ąík)¬≤ = r¬≤.
- If given a messy expanded equation, **complete the square** (for x and for y separately) to find the center and radius.
  - Example: x¬≤ ‚ąí 4x + y¬≤ + 8y = 2 ‚Üí (x‚ąí2)¬≤ + (y+4)¬≤ = 22, so center (2,‚ąí4), radius ‚ąö22.

---

## 6. Power of a Point (algebraic view)

This is the same theorem already in [[02_Angles_Similarity_Circles]], but here's how to find where a line crosses a circle using algebra: substitute the line's equation into the circle's equation, giving a quadratic ‚ÄĒ its two roots are the two crossing points' coordinates.

> ūüí° **Competition point:** Combining a line equation and a circle equation always reduces to solving ONE quadratic. If the discriminant (see [[12_Equations_Algebra_Factoring]]) is zero, the line is tangent to the circle (touches at exactly one point).
