Divisors and Multiples

Source: AOPS Introduction to Number Theory В§1.5 "Divisors" and Ch.3 В§3.6 (sums & differences of multiples)

  • Divisor (Factor): d is a divisor of n when n Г· d is a whole number (no remainder). "Divisor" and "factor" mean the same thing.
    Example: divisors of 12 вҶ’ 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12 (six of them).
    Divisors come in pairs: 12 = 1Г—12 = 2Г—6 = 3Г—4. Test from the smallest number up and you'll never miss one.
  • Four ways to say the same thing (for d вү  0): "n is a multiple of d" = "n is divisible by d" = "d is a divisor of n" = "d divides n."
  • Multiple: a number you reach by multiplying. Multiples of 3 вҶ’ 3, 6, 9, 12, вҖҰ (never ending).
  • Every integer (except 0) is a divisor of itself (n = nВ·1), and every integer is a divisor of 0 (0 Г· n = 0).
  • You only need to test 1 up to n for divisors: a number bigger than n can't divide n (e.g. 0 < 14/36 < 1 means 36 is not a divisor of 14).
  • Divisibility is "transitive": if a divides b and b divides c, then a divides c. (Every divisor of 6 is automatically a divisor of 18, because 6 divides 18.)

A key competition tool вҖ” sums and differences of multiples. If n divides both a and b, then n divides a + b and a вҲ’ b.

  • Why: write a = xn and b = yn; then a Вұ b = (x Вұ y)n, which is n times an integer, hence a multiple of n.
  • Restated: a common divisor of two numbers is also a divisor of their sum and of their difference. (This one fact powers the Euclidean Algorithm in В§6.)
Competition point: A number with an odd number of divisors must be a perfect square (36 = 6ВІ вҶ’ 9 divisors), because only a square has an unpaired divisor (the вҲҡ). Negative integers can be divisors too (вҲ’2 is a divisor of 6), though competition problems usually mean positive ones.

Primes, Composites & Divisibility Rules

Source: AOPS Introduction to Number Theory В§2.3 "Identifying Primes" (Sieve of Eratosthenes) and the divisibility-rule sections of Ch.2

  • Prime: a natural number (> 1) whose only divisors are 1 and itself (2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, вҖҰ). Composite: more than two divisors (4, 6, 8, 9, вҖҰ). 1 is neither prime nor composite. 2 is the only even prime.
  • Sieve of Eratosthenes (finding all primes up to N): list 2, 3, 4, вҖҰ, N; circle 2 and cross out every larger multiple of 2; circle the next uncrossed number (3) and cross out its multiples; repeat. Whatever's circled is prime. (Named for Eratosthenes, ~230 BC.)
  • There are infinitely many primes (Euclid's proof): if there were only finitely many pвӮҒ,вҖҰ,pвӮҷ, then pвӮҒpвӮӮвҖҰpвӮҷ + 1 leaves remainder 1 when divided by each pбөў, so it has a new prime divisor вҖ” contradiction.

Divisibility rules:

  • Divisible by 2: last digit is 0, 2, 4, 6, 8 (even)
  • Divisible by 3: the sum of the digits is a multiple of 3 (e.g. 123 вҶ’ 1+2+3 = 6 вң“)
  • Divisible by 4: the last two digits form a multiple of 4
  • Divisible by 5: last digit is 0 or 5
  • Divisible by 9: the sum of the digits is a multiple of 9 (a stronger version of the rule for 3)
  • Divisible by 11: (sum of odd-position digits) вҲ’ (sum of even-position digits) is 0 or a multiple of 11 (Sec)
Competition point: Problems like "digit sum is a multiple of 7" (вҶ’ AP 2021 Q26 below) start from this idea.

Prime Factorisation вҖ” Sec 1

Source: AOPS Introduction to Number Theory Ch.4 (Prime Factorization / Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic)

GCD and LCM вҖ” Sec 1

Source: AOPS Introduction to Number Theory Ch.3 (Multiples and Divisors вҖ” common divisors) & Ch.6 (GCD/LCM from prime factorization)

  • GCD (Greatest Common Divisor): the biggest shared divisor. From prime factors, take each shared prime with the smaller power.
  • LCM (Lowest Common Multiple): the smallest shared multiple. Take every prime with the larger power.
  • Example: 12 = 2ВІГ—3, 18 = 2Г—3ВІ вҶ’ GCD = 2Г—3 = 6, LCM = 2ВІГ—3ВІ = 36
  • Golden formula: GCD Г— LCM = product of the two numbers вҶ’ 6Г—36 = 216 = 12Г—18 вң“
  • Letting variables (must-know): if GCD = k, then A = kВ·a and B = kВ·b (where a and b are coprime).
    Then A + B = k(a+b) and LCM = kВ·aВ·b вҶ’ the condition breaks apart nicely.
Competition point: When both GCD and LCM are given, always set A = ka, B = kb (вҶ’ Mock Q2-21: A+B = 2024, LCM = 3795).

Euclidean Algorithm вҖ” Sec, advanced

Source: AOPS Introduction to Number Theory В§3.7 "The Euclidean Algorithm"

  • Why it works: a common divisor of two numbers also divides their difference (В§1). So the pairs (a, b) and (aвҲ’b, b) have exactly the same common divisors вҖ” and therefore the same GCD.
    Subtraction form: gcd(m, n) = gcd(m вҲ’ n, n) for m > n. Example: gcd(990, 720) = gcd(270, 720) = gcd(270, 450) = gcd(270, 180) = gcd(90, 180) = gcd(90, 90) = 90.
  • Faster division form: gcd(a, b) = gcd(b, a mod b) вҖ” replace the pair by the remainder of dividing the bigger by the smaller, repeat until one is 0.
    Example: gcd(48, 18) вҶ’ gcd(18, 12) вҶ’ gcd(12, 6) вҶ’ gcd(6, 0) = 6.
  • Why useful: it finds the GCD of large numbers (too big to prime-factorise) in just a few steps вҖ” e.g. gcd(819, 504) without factoring either one.
Competition point: Word problems that ask for the largest equal-size piece (e.g. two rectangles of area 1086 and 828 sharing an integer width вҶ’ the width is a common divisor, so the largest is gcd(1086, 828)) are Euclidean-algorithm problems in disguise.

More Tools (extra divisibility & GCD/LCM tricks)

Source: divisibility rules for 7/8/13/etc. and conditional divisor-counting extend AOPS Introduction to Number Theory Ch.2вҖ“5; some (fraction GCD/LCM) are standard competition results.

  • GCD/LCM scaling rule: if you multiply both numbers by the same n, then GCD(na, nb) = nГ—GCD(a,b) and LCM(na, nb) = nГ—LCM(a,b). Spot an obvious common factor first, then only prime-factorise the leftover (smaller) numbers.
    Example: GCD(24, 36) вҖ” 24 = 4Г—6, 36 = 4Г—9 вҶ’ GCD = 4Г—GCD(6,9) = 4Г—3 = 12
  • Divisibility by 6: divisible by 6 exactly when divisible by both 2 and 3. This works because 2 and 3 are coprime. Careful: 18 is divisible by both 2 and 6, but that does not mean it's divisible by 12 вҖ” 2 and 6 are not coprime (they share the factor 2), so the trick breaks.
  • More last-digits rules: divisible by 8 вҶ’ last 3 digits divisible by 8. By 16 вҶ’ last 4 digits. By 25 вҶ’ last 2 digits. By 125 вҶ’ last 3 digits. By 625 вҶ’ last 4 digits.
  • Divisibility by 7 (advanced trick): double the last digit, subtract it from the rest of the number, repeat if needed вҖ” divisible by 7 iff the result is.
    Example: is 616 divisible by 7? вҶ’ 61 вҲ’ (6Г—2) = 49 вҶ’ yes, divisible by 7.
  • Divisibility by 13 (advanced trick): remove the last digit, subtract 9Г— that digit from what's left, repeat if needed.
  • Counting divisors with a condition: for "even divisors" / "multiples of m" / "perfect square divisors" etc., factor out the required piece first, then apply the (power+1) rule to what remains.
    Example: 72 = 2ВіГ—3ВІ вҶ’ even divisors вҶ’ factor out one 2, leaving 2ВІГ—3ВІ вҶ’ (2+1)(2+1) = 9 even divisors
  • Perfect-square divisors: a divisor is a perfect square only if every prime's power in it is even.
  • LCM or GCD вҖ” which one? Things recurring/coinciding again (flashing lights, buses meeting, gears lining up) вҶ’ LCM of the cycle lengths. Things being cut/split into equal pieces вҶ’ GCD.
  • GCD/LCM of fractions (Sec): LCM(a/b, c/d) = LCM(a,c) / GCD(b,d), and GCD(a/b, c/d) = GCD(a,c) / LCM(b,d). Same prime-power idea also works for GCD/LCM of algebraic terms (treat each variable's exponent like a prime's power).
