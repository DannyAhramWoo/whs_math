## 1. What an Angle Is, and Measuring It

> üìñ *Source: AOPS Introduction to Geometry ¬ß2.1 "What is an Angle?", ¬ß2.2 "Measuring Angles"*

- **Angle:** two rays sharing a common origin. The shared origin is the **vertex**; the two rays are the **sides**.
  - We write ‚à†XOY with the **vertex in the middle** (‚à†XOY or ‚à†YOX, but never ‚à†XYO). When only one angle can be meant, just ‚à†O.
  - When two lines cross at P, "‚à†P" is ambiguous ‚Äî you must name three points (‚à†APC, ‚à†BPC, ‚Ä¶) to say *which* angle.
- **Measuring ‚Äî degrees.** A full circle is **360¬∞**; a half-circle (**semicircle**) is 180¬∞. A protractor is a semicircle split into 180 equal degrees.
- **Naming angles by size:** **acute** < 90¬∞, **right** = 90¬∞, **obtuse** between 90¬∞ and 180¬∞, **straight** = 180¬∞, **reflex** > 180¬∞. Two lines that meet at a right angle are **perpendicular** (‚ä•).
- **The "fraction of a circle" view (very useful).** An angle's measure = the fraction of a full circle it cuts off, times 360¬∞.
  - ¬º of a circle = 90¬∞, ‚Öì = 120¬∞, 1/12 = 30¬∞, 1/8 = 45¬∞.

> üí° **Competition point:** The "fraction of a circle" idea turns pie-chart / clock / pizza-slice problems into a one-line calculation: (fraction) √ó 360¬∞. A clock's hour marks are `360¬∞/12 = 30¬∞` apart.

## 2. Angle Chasing (straight, vertical, supplementary, complementary)

> üìñ *Source: AOPS Introduction to Geometry ¬ß2.3 "Straight and Vertical Angles"*

- **Straight angle:** a straight line is an angle of **180¬∞** (it cuts off exactly half a circle).
- **Supplementary angles:** two angles that add to **180¬∞**. Any two adjacent angles on a straight line are supplementary.
  - Example: if two angles sit on a line and one is 55¬∞, the other is `180¬∞ ‚àí 55¬∞ = 125¬∞`.
- **Complementary angles:** two angles that add to **90¬∞**.
- **Vertical (opposite) angles are equal.** When two lines cross, the angles directly across from each other are equal.
  - *Why (proof):* ‚à†MPN = 180¬∞ ‚àí ‚à†NPO (straight line), and ‚à†LPO = 180¬∞ ‚àí ‚à†NPO (straight line), so ‚à†MPN = ‚à†LPO. The proof never used the actual number ‚Äî vertical angles are *always* equal.

> **Concept:** When you can't compute the target angle directly, **find whatever angles you can first** ‚Äî a straight line or a crossing point almost always hands you one, and that unlocks the rest.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Warning:** An *example* is not a *proof*. Checking one case (e.g. "when it's 72¬∞ it works") does not prove a statement always holds.

## 3. Parallel Lines Cut by a Transversal

> üìñ *Source: AOPS Introduction to Geometry ¬ß2.4 "Parallel Lines"*

- **Parallel lines** never meet; we write AB ‚à• CD. Little arrows on the lines mark them parallel.
- When a **transversal** (crossing line) cuts two parallel lines, all **8 angles fall into just two sizes**:
  - a group of **four equal angles**, and another group of **four equal angles**, and the two group-sizes are **supplementary** (add to 180¬∞).
- So every pair is either **equal** or **supplementary** ‚Äî that's all you really need. (The formal names ‚Äî corresponding, alternate interior/exterior, co-interior ‚Äî are nice to know but not worth memorising; just see which angles match and which sum to 180¬∞.)

**Worked example.** m ‚à• n, one angle marked 113¬∞. Every angle equals **113¬∞** or its supplement **67¬∞** (`180¬∞ ‚àí 113¬∞`). Pick each angle's size by whether it's in the same group as the 113¬∞ or the other group.

> üí° **Competition point:** The instant you see parallel lines, copy the one known angle to **every** matching position in the figure (equal) and mark the supplements (180¬∞ ‚àí it). Angle problems collapse once every angle is labelled.

## 4. Angles in a Triangle (the 180¬∞ rule)

> üìñ *Source: AOPS Introduction to Geometry ¬ß2.5 "Angles in a Triangle"*

- **The three interior angles of any triangle add to 180¬∞.** This is one of the most-used facts in all of geometry.
  - *Why (proof sketch):* through vertex A draw a line parallel to BC. The two "extra" angles it makes equal ‚à†B and ‚à†C (alternate angles), and together with ‚à†A they form a straight angle ‚Üí ‚à†A + ‚à†B + ‚à†C = 180¬∞.
- **Turning words into equations.** Most triangle word problems: name the unknown angles with a variable, write "they sum to 180¬∞," solve.
  - Example: one angle is twice another, the third is 54¬∞. Let the small one be x, the other 2x ‚Üí `54¬∞ + x + 2x = 180¬∞` ‚Üí x = 42¬∞. Angles are 42¬∞, 54¬∞, 84¬∞; smallest = **42¬∞**.

> **Concept:** Seeing "180¬∞" should make you look for a **useful line** ‚Äî often a parallel line, because parallel lines hand out equal angles. That's the trick behind the proof above.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Warning:** Your *last* step should always be to check you answered the actual question asked (here: the *smallest* angle, not just x).

## 5. Exterior Angles (and the 360¬∞ result)

> üìñ *Source: AOPS Introduction to Geometry ¬ß2.6 "Exterior Angles"*

- **Exterior angle** = the angle formed by extending one side of a triangle past a vertex.
- **Exterior Angle Theorem:** an exterior angle equals the **sum of the two non-adjacent (far) interior angles**.
  - *Why:* at vertex C, interior ‚à†ACB and exterior x sit on a straight line, so `‚à†ACB + x = 180¬∞`; but also `‚à†A + ‚à†B + ‚à†ACB = 180¬∞`, so `x = ‚à†A + ‚à†B`.
  - Example: interior angles 35¬∞ and 79¬∞ ‚Üí the far exterior angle is `35¬∞ + 79¬∞ = 114¬∞`.
- **Beautiful result ‚Äî the three exterior angles of a triangle add to 360¬∞.**
  - Each exterior angle = sum of its two far interior angles, so x + y + z = 2(‚à†A + ‚à†B + ‚à†C) = 2(180¬∞) = **360¬∞**. (This generalises: the exterior angles of *any* convex polygon sum to 360¬∞.)

> üí° **Competition point:** The exterior-angle theorem lets you skip a step ‚Äî read the far angle directly as a sum instead of computing the interior angle first (‚Üí RI 2024 Q20: sum the 5 tip angles of a star by chasing exterior angles).

- **Isosceles triangle:** if two sides are equal, the **two base angles are equal** (and vice versa) ‚Äî a constant source of "hidden" equal angles.

## 6. Congruence, Similarity & Triangle/Quadrilateral Properties ‚Äî *Sec 1*

> üìñ *Source: AOPS Introduction to Geometry Ch.3 (Congruent Triangles, ¬ß3.8 Summary) & Ch.5 (Similar Triangles); "same base / same altitude" area idea from Ch.4*

**Congruent = exactly the same.** Two figures are **congruent** if you can slide, spin, and/or flip one to land exactly on the other. For triangles, four tests prove congruence:

- **SSS** ‚Äî three sides equal.
- **SAS** ‚Äî two sides and the angle *between* them equal.
- **ASA** ‚Äî two angles and the side *between* them equal.
- **AAS** ‚Äî two angles and a *non-included* side equal.
- **CPCTC** ‚Äî once triangles are congruent, **C**orresponding **P**arts of **C**ongruent **T**riangles are **C**ongruent: every matching side and angle is equal. This is how one congruence unlocks many equal lengths/angles.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Warning ‚Äî the SSA trap:** SSA (two sides + a non-included angle) is **NOT** a valid congruence test. The same three measurements can give two different triangles (this is the "ambiguous case" ‚Äî e.g. AB = 13, BC = 10, ‚à†A = 40¬∞ has *two* possible triangles).

**Isosceles & equilateral (a constant source of equal angles):**

- **Isosceles:** if two sides are equal, the two **base angles** are equal ‚Äî and the converse: equal base angles force equal sides. (AB = AC ‚ü∫ ‚à†B = ‚à†C.)
- **Equilateral:** all three sides equal ‚ü∫ all three angles equal ‚ü∫ every angle is **60¬∞**.
- *Trick:* dropping a segment from the apex of an isosceles triangle to the midpoint of the base splits it into two congruent right triangles ‚Äî instant symmetry.

**Similar = same shape, scaled size.** Matching angles are equal and matching sides share one ratio **k** (the scale factor). Three tests:

- **AA** ‚Äî two angles equal (the one you'll use most; the third angle is then automatic).
- **SAS similarity** ‚Äî two sides in the same ratio with the included angle equal.
- **SSS similarity** ‚Äî all three sides in the same constant ratio.
  - Example: ‚ñ≥ABC with sides 7, 4, 5 and ‚ñ≥DEF with sides 14, 8, 10 ‚Üí every side doubles, so AB/DE = AC/DF = BC/EF = 1/2 ‚Üí ‚ñ≥ABC ~ ‚ñ≥DEF, and therefore ‚à†A=‚à†D, ‚à†B=‚à†E, ‚à†C=‚à†F.

- **Scale factor k ‚Üí area k¬≤, volume k¬≥.** Side ratio k means perimeters in ratio k, **areas in ratio k¬≤**, volumes in ratio k¬≥.
  - Sides √ó2 ‚Üí area √ó4, volume √ó8.

**Area shortcuts (very competition-relevant):**

- **Same height ‚Üí area ratio = base ratio.** Two triangles with equal heights compare by their bases alone.
- **Same base and same height ‚Üí equal area**, even if the shapes look different (a triangle "slides" its apex along a line parallel to the base without changing area).
- **Midpoint theorem:** the segment joining midpoints of two sides is **half the third side** and **parallel** to it.
- **Parallelogram:** opposite sides equal; diagonals **bisect each other**.
- **Trapezium area:** (top + bottom) √ó height √∑ 2.

> üí° **Competition point:** To compare two areas, hunt for **similar triangles** (use the k¬≤ rule) or **shared height/base** ‚Äî never measure. Proving a small congruence with SSS/SAS/ASA/AAS, then applying CPCTC, is the workhorse for "show these two lengths are equal" problems (‚Üí RI 2022 Q02: from a parallelogram's area, find triangle ABC).

## 7. Circle Properties ‚Äî *Sec 2*

> üìñ *Source: AOPS Introduction to Geometry ¬ß11.4 (Circle basics), Ch.12 ¬ß12.6 Summary (Circles and Angles) & Ch.13 ¬ß13.3 Summary (Power of a Point)*

**Vocabulary.** A **circle** is all points a fixed distance (the **radius** r) from a fixed **center**. A **chord** joins two points on the circle; a **diameter** is a chord through the center (length 2r). A **secant** is a line hitting the circle at two points; a **tangent** touches at exactly one point. An **arc** is a piece of the circle; a **central angle** (vertex at the center) equals the measure of the arc it cuts off.

**Basic size formulas.** **Circumference = œÄd = 2œÄr**, and **area = œÄr¬≤**. A **radius perpendicular to a chord bisects that chord** (and vice versa) ‚Äî a reflex move for chord problems.

**The angle rules (this is the whole chapter in one place).** Where an angle's vertex sits decides the rule:

- **Vertex ON the circle ‚Äî inscribed angle = half its arc.** An angle formed by two chords from a point on the circle equals **half the arc it cuts off**: ‚à†PQR = (arc PR)/2.
  - **Consequence 1:** any angle inscribed in a **semicircle is 90¬∞** (Thales ‚Äî the arc is a full semicircle = 180¬∞, half is 90¬∞).
  - **Consequence 2:** two angles inscribed in (standing on) the **same arc are equal**.
  - **Consequence 3:** a **central angle is twice** any inscribed angle on the same arc.
- **Vertex INSIDE ‚Äî average of the two arcs.** Two chords crossing inside the circle make an angle equal to **half the sum** of the two arcs they cut off: ‚à† = (arc‚ÇÅ + arc‚ÇÇ)/2.
- **Vertex OUTSIDE ‚Äî half the difference.** Two secants (or a tangent + secant, or two tangents) meeting outside make an angle equal to **half the difference** of the far and near arcs: ‚à† = (far arc ‚àí near arc)/2.
- **Tangent‚Äìchord angle:** an angle between a tangent and a chord at the point of tangency = **half the intercepted arc**.

**Tangent & radius facts:**

- A tangent is **perpendicular to the radius** drawn to the point of tangency (and the converse: a line through a circle point perpendicular to that radius must be tangent). "Draw the radius to a tangent point" is a reflex move.
- From an outside point you can draw **two tangent segments, and they're equal in length**.

**Cyclic quadrilateral:** if all 4 corners lie on a circle, **opposite angles add to 180¬∞**.

**Circular segment area** (the region between a chord and its arc): compute the **sector** (pie slice) cut off by the two radii to the chord's ends, then **subtract the triangle** formed by those two radii and the chord. Segment = sector ‚àí triangle.

**Power of a Point (Sec 2, advanced but very useful).** For any line through a point P meeting the circle at U and V, the product **(PU)(PV) is constant** ‚Äî the "power" of P.

- **P inside** (two chords cross at X): (XA)(XB) = (XC)(XD).
- **P outside** (two secants): (PB)(PA) = (PD)(PC) ‚Äî *the same outside point P must appear in every segment.*
- **Tangent from outside:** (tangent PT)¬≤ = (PB)(PA) for any secant through P.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Warning:** In the outside-point version, the shared point must be in *all four* segments. Writing (BC)(BA) = (DC)(DE) is wrong; the correct pairing keeps C common: (CD)(CE) = (CB)(CA).

> üí° **Competition point:** When a circle appears, **draw radii to every key point** and hunt for a right angle (tangent) or a diameter (Thales) ‚Äî those right angles unlock everything. If a segment **stops in the middle of a circle**, extend it to the circle and reach for Power of a Point (‚Üí AP 2021 Q10: area of a circle around 7 unit squares; AP 2021 Q24: two perpendicular chords).

## 8. Pythagoras, Special Triangles & Area Cutting ‚Äî *Sec 1*

> üìñ *Source: AOPS Introduction to Geometry ¬ß6.2 (Two Special Right Triangles), ¬ß6.3 (Pythagorean Triples), ¬ß6.5‚òÖ (Heron's Formula), ¬ß6.7 Summary, and Ch.10 ¬ß10.3 (Triangle Inequality)*

- **Pythagoras' theorem:** in a **right-angled triangle**, **a¬≤ + b¬≤ = c¬≤**, where **c is the hypotenuse** (longest side, opposite the right angle).
- **Pythagorean triples (memorise!):** whole-number sides that fit a¬≤+b¬≤=c¬≤ ‚Äî **3-4-5**, **5-12-13**, **7-24-25**, **8-15-17**, **9-40-41**. Any multiple works too (6-8-10, 9-12-15). Spotting one skips all calculation.
  - Legs 3 and 4 ‚Üí hypotenuse **5**, no working. (Note {1,2,3} is *not* a triple ‚Äî you can't make the third from the other two.)
- **Acute / right / obtuse by the Pythagorean comparison:** for a triangle with longest side *c*, compare c¬≤ with a¬≤+b¬≤. If **c¬≤ = a¬≤+b¬≤** it's right; if **c¬≤ < a¬≤+b¬≤** the angle opposite c is **acute** (whole triangle acute); if **c¬≤ > a¬≤+b¬≤** that angle is **obtuse**. (Pythagoras is just the boundary case.)
- **Longest side faces the largest angle.** In any triangle, the biggest angle is opposite the longest side and the smallest angle opposite the shortest side (‚à†A ‚â• ‚à†B ‚â• ‚à†C ‚ü∫ a ‚â• b ‚â• c). Handy for ordering sides/angles without measuring.

**Two special right triangles (know the side ratios cold):**

- **45-45-90 (isosceles right):** the two legs are equal and the **hypotenuse = leg √ó ‚àö2**. (Proof: leg¬≤ + leg¬≤ = 2¬∑leg¬≤ = hyp¬≤.)
  - Leg 5 ‚Üí hypotenuse 5‚àö2. Hypotenuse 6 ‚Üí each leg 6/‚àö2 = 3‚àö2.
- **30-60-90:** sides are in ratio **1 : ‚àö3 : 2** (short leg : long leg : hypotenuse). The short leg faces the 30¬∞ angle, the long leg faces 60¬∞, the hypotenuse faces 90¬∞.
  - Hypotenuse 12 with a 30¬∞ angle ‚Üí short leg 6, long leg 6‚àö3.
  - *These come from cutting an equilateral triangle in half with an altitude* ‚Äî which is also how you derive the **equilateral-triangle area = (‚àö3/4)¬∑s¬≤**.

> üí° **Competition point:** The instant you see a **30¬∞, 60¬∞, 120¬∞, or 45¬∞** angle in a figure, look for a hidden 30-60-90 or 45-45-90 triangle ‚Äî you can then read off the sides with no trig.

**Heron's Formula (area from three sides, no height needed):**

- **[ABC] = ‚àö(s(s‚àía)(s‚àíb)(s‚àíc))**, where **s = (a+b+c)/2** is the semiperimeter.
  - Example: sides 13, 14, 15 ‚Üí s = 21 ‚Üí area = ‚àö(21¬∑8¬∑7¬∑6) = ‚àö7056 = **84**.
- Derived by dropping an altitude and applying Pythagoras twice ‚Äî the reason the area is fixed once you fix all three sides (SSS congruence).

**Triangle Inequality (when three lengths can even form a triangle):**

- Each side must be **less than the sum** of the other two: a+b>c, a+c>b, b+c>a. (Equivalently, each side is **greater than the difference** of the other two.)
  - Sides 7 and 13 ‚Üí the third side x must satisfy **6 < x < 20**.

**Area cutting:** split a complex shape into simple **triangles and rectangles**, then add or subtract their areas.

> üí° **Competition point:** For a shaded region, think "**whole minus holes**" ‚Äî compute one big easy area, subtract the unwanted pieces. When paper is **folded**, the two layers are **congruent**, handing you hidden equal lengths ‚Äî set up a Pythagoras equation on the crease (‚Üí Mock Q1-27: fold so A lands on C).

## 9. More Tools (advanced / competition extras)

> üìñ *Source: AOPS Introduction to Geometry Ch.7 (Special Parts of a Triangle ‚Äî centers), Ch.8 (Quadrilaterals), Ch.9 (Polygons), Ch.11 (sector area), plus standard competition results (Ptolemy). These go beyond the core chapters above.*

- **Right-triangle shortcuts:** besides SSS/SAS/ASA/AAS, right triangles also have **HL** (hypotenuse + one leg) and **LL** (two legs) for **congruence** ‚Äî and the matching **HL similarity** and **LL similarity** tests when the parts are in a constant ratio instead of equal.
- **A median equal to half the side it hits ‚ü∫ right triangle** (that side is then the hypotenuse and its midpoint is the circumcenter) ‚Äî the converse of the "circumcenter = hypotenuse midpoint" fact.
- **Four triangle centers** (Ch.7):
  - **Centroid** ‚Äî the three **medians** meet here, cutting each median **2:1** from the vertex; splits the triangle into **6 equal-area** pieces.
  - **Incenter** ‚Äî the three **angle bisectors** meet here, equidistant from the 3 *sides* (center of the incircle, radius r). **Area = r √ó s** (s = semiperimeter).
  - **Circumcenter** ‚Äî the three **perpendicular bisectors** meet here, equidistant from the 3 *vertices* (center of the circumcircle, radius R). For a **right triangle**, the circumcenter is the **midpoint of the hypotenuse**, so **R = ¬Ω ¬∑ hypotenuse**.
  - **Orthocenter** ‚Äî where the three **altitudes** meet.
- **Angle Bisector Theorem:** if AD bisects ‚à†A (D on BC), then **AB/BD = AC/DC** ‚Äî the bisector splits the opposite side in the ratio of the two adjacent sides.
- **Altitude-to-hypotenuse (right triangle):** the altitude from the right angle to the hypotenuse at D gives three "geometric mean" facts: **BD¬≤ = AD¬∑DC**, **AB¬≤ = AD¬∑AC**, **CB¬≤ = CD¬∑CA**.
- **Ptolemy's Theorem** (cyclic quadrilateral ABCD): **AC ¬∑ BD = AB¬∑CD + AD¬∑BC**.
- **Incircle/circumcircle radii:** **r = Area/s**; for a right triangle **r = (leg‚ÇÅ + leg‚ÇÇ ‚àí hypotenuse)/2**; and **R = abc/(4¬∑Area)**.
- **Incircle tangent lengths:** the incircle touches the three sides so that the two tangent segments from each vertex are equal, giving **(distance from a vertex to a touch-point) = s ‚àí (opposite side)** ‚Äî i.e. s‚àía, s‚àíb, s‚àíc from the three vertices. Very handy for "find the segment" problems.
- **Special quadrilaterals** (Ch.8):
  - **Rhombus:** area = (d‚ÇÅ √ó d‚ÇÇ)/2, diagonals perpendicular.
  - **Isosceles trapezoid:** base angles equal in pairs, legs equal, diagonals equal (any one forces the others).
  - **Trapezoid median** (midpoints of the legs): parallel to both bases, **median = (base‚ÇÅ+base‚ÇÇ)/2**, **Area = height √ó median**.
  - **Kite:** two pairs of *consecutive* equal sides; diagonals perpendicular.
  > üí° **Competition point:** for true/false "is every rhombus a rectangle?" traps, remember **square ‚äÇ rectangle ‚äÇ parallelogram** and **square ‚äÇ rhombus ‚äÇ parallelogram** ‚Äî a rhombus is a rectangle only if it's a square.
- **Sector formulas** (Ch.11): arc length = (angle/360) √ó 2œÄr, sector area = (angle/360) √ó œÄr¬≤.
- **Regular polygons** (Ch.9): hexagon area = 6 √ó (‚àö3/4) √ó side¬≤, long diagonal = 2 √ó side; octagon area = 2(1+‚àö2) √ó side¬≤.
- **Belt/rope around touching circles:** total length = straight tangent parts (each = sum of the two radii it touches) + curved arcs, and **the curved parts always sum to exactly one full circle**, no matter how many circles.
- **Symmetry:** a regular n-gon has n lines of symmetry and **rotational symmetry angle = 360¬∞/n**.
